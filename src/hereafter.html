Product Development

PROBLEM SPACE

As university students ourselves, my team identified mental health as a prevalent challenge among peers. We wanted to create a tool that facilitates mindfulness and emotional awareness through journaling.

USER INTERVIEWS

We started off by interviewing 8 students from York University and Sheridan College who use or considered journaling as a self-reflection method.

Insights were also sought from a psychotherapist for information on navigating sensitive topics responsibly while designing a mental health focused product.

To ensure we approached the topic of mental health thoughtfully:
Interviews were conducted over Zoom for a week, each session lasting 30 minutes each.
I was involved in developing a script with open-ended questions to explore participants' habits, challenges, and motivations.

Our Curiosities

In total, we prepared 17 questions for the students, split into two segments.

Some questions we prepared for the students included:
a) Methods and Outlets
How do you keep track of or document your daily experiences and feelings? Can you walk me through that process?
What tools or other outlets do you utilise in reflecting your emotions? (friends, families, notes, social media?)
b) Ideals and Painpoints
What does your ideal reflection/coping method look like?
Are there any unique features in your method of documentation that you like/dislike?
Do you think self-reflecting will help your mental health?

Interview Answers

N.L: How do you keep track of or document your daily experiences and feelings? Can you walk me through that process?
C.A: I meditate at night, if I don't pass out from exhaustion. I don’t formally document anything or follow a routine, but I try to stay aware of my mood choices.
N.L: What could be better about your current experience?
T.N: Identifying my emotions can be so hard sometimes. It'd be nice to have a way to just list everything out on a blank page and have it sorted for me as a starting point—like putting everything up on a wall without needing to organize it myself, or better yet, having it already organized for me.
N.L: Do you actively seek a solution to your problem? Or go with the flow?
F.B: I mostly go with the flow. I am more reactive than proactive.

So,,, what now?

Through a Prioritization Grid, we brainstormed potential solutions, considering both Big Bets (aka high-risk, high-reward ideas) and No-Brainers (simple, high-impact solutions). After discussing the options, we voted on the top 3 priorities that would deliver the most value. This process led to a clear problem statement that focused on addressing the primary painpoints our user had.

Usability Testing

I recruited two participants through cold emailing—one who journals often and another who doesn’t. Each usability test lasted around 20 minutes over Zoom, where participants shared their screens while completing the task of writing a journal entry in a mid-fidelity prototype. I logged successes and failures at each step and asked follow-up questions about ease of use, prompts, editing entries, likes/dislikes, and overall ratings.

Final Outcomes

Here:after, as a whole, offers a simple and efficient journaling experience tailored for students with limited time. Its design prioritizes accessibility and makes it easier for users to understand and express their emotional patterns.

This project was a pivotal experience that taught me how to understand users and their needs. Designing Here:after, a mental health app aimed at supporting self-reflection, pushed me to develop stronger research and design skills. One of the most impactful lessons I learned was the power of asking thoughtful, open-ended questions and adapting in the moment, even if conversations veered off script. These unscripted moments often revealed profound insights, allowing us to dive deeper into the needs of our users.

Through user testing, we uncovered a clear preference for simplicity—users wanted a clean, distraction-free interface that seamlessly guided them through every step. The project’s practical, user-centered approach earned us the UX Tie-Winner award at the RGD 2023 Student Design Awards. Reflecting on the experience, I saw opportunities for even greater impact. In the future, our group considered integrating additional mental health resources like meditation or breathing exercises, as well as adding drawing features—something users specifically expressed interest in. Ultimately, personalization was a crucial aspect of what made Here:after so meaningful to our users.
